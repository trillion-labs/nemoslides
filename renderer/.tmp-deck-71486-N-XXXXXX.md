# Why now: the market rewards density, speed, and shared economics

::left::

### What is changing
- Customers expect **same-day / next-day reliability** at lower delivery fees
- Merchant demand is shifting toward **flexible, multi-channel fulfillment**
- Last-mile economics are getting harder as:
  - stop density varies by market
  - labor and fuel remain volatile
  - service failures quickly erode NPS and retention

### What that means for MoveIt
- Expanding alone would require **material network capex**
- Scale benefits come only after reaching **route density**
- The risk is building too much infrastructure before volume fully matures

::right::

### Why FastFreight is the right partner now
- Established **terminal footprint and middle-mile expertise**
- Existing carrier relationships and operating discipline
- Complementary customer base and shipment flows

### The strategic opening
- Combine MoveIt's **demand, product, and customer experience**
- With FastFreight's **physical network and logistics execution**
- To create a **shared last-mile platform** that reaches break-even faster and scales with less duplication

# Partnership fit: complementary strengths, limited overlap

| MoveIt brings | FastFreight brings | Joint advantage |
|---|---|---|
| Strong urban delivery demand and merchant relationships | Regional terminal footprint and linehaul network | Faster volume ramp in target metros |
| Consumer-facing app, routing UX, real-time tracking | Operational know-how in dispatch, fleet, and compliance | Better customer experience with operational reliability |
| Data on demand patterns, delivery windows, and drop density | Carrier relationships, depot processes, and labor management | More efficient route planning and capacity utilization |
| Brand momentum in convenience and speed | Proven B2B logistics credibility | Broader commercial reach across SMB and enterprise |
| Digital integration and order orchestration | Physical execution at scale | End-to-end service without duplicating fixed cost |

### Bottom line
This is not two companies doing the same thing together.  
It is a pairing of **digital demand density + physical logistics capability**.

# Why a joint venture is stronger than expanding alone

| Option | Benefits | Constraints | Likely outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MoveIt expands alone** | Full control, unified brand | Must build depots, recruit carriers, and absorb startup losses market by market | Slower rollout, higher capex, later break-even |
| **FastFreight expands alone** | Leverages existing logistics assets | Needs consumer-grade product, merchant demand density, and delivery experience layer | Underutilized network and slower parcel density ramp |
| **Joint venture** | Shares assets, speeds launches, aligns incentives, pools volume | Requires clear governance and data-sharing rules | Best path to scale with stronger unit economics |

### Why the JV wins
- **9–12 months faster** market launch versus stand-alone buildouts
- **~$45M avoided duplicated capex** over 3 years
- **20–25% higher route density** in overlapping metros
- **6–8 margin points better** by Year 3 versus stand-alone expansion cases

# Proposed operating model

### Structure
- Form **JVCo**, a ring-fenced operating entity focused on shared last-mile services
- Recommended starting point: **50/50 ownership**, with reserved matters requiring joint approval
- Launch in markets where both parties have strong demand and network adjacency

### Contributions
- **MoveIt**
  - Demand generation and merchant integrations
  - Routing / customer experience product layer
  - Brand, tracking, support standards, service design
- **FastFreight**
  - Terminal access and linehaul injection
  - Local carrier operations, fleet processes, compliance
  - Dispatch discipline and network planning support

### How JVCo works day to day
- JVCo owns the **market P&L**, service levels, and local launch execution
- Shared operating playbook with:
  - unified service KPIs
  - open-book transfer pricing
  - common planning cadence for capacity and expansion
- Market-level GMs accountable for **cost, service, and density**

# Expected economics: attractive growth with faster payback

### Primary value drivers
- **Shared infrastructure:** fewer new depots and sort investments
- **Denser routes:** lower cost per stop and better courier utilization
- **Joint procurement and fleet efficiency:** lower operating cost

# Rollout plan: phased, gated, and measurable

| Phase | Timing | Scope | Success gates |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Design & diligence** | 0–90 days | Finalize JV terms, operating blueprint, tech/data plan, pilot economics | Signed term sheet, target market list, integration readiness |
| **2. Pilot launch** | Months 4–9 | Launch 3 metros with shared service levels and joint staffing model | On-time delivery, NPS, cost/stop, courier utilization |
| **3. Controlled scale-up** | Months 10–18 | Expand to 8 metros; add enterprise merchant programs | Positive contribution margin in launch cohorts |
| **4. Network buildout** | Months 19–36 | Reach 15 metros; standardize playbook and procurement | EBITDA-positive network and repeatable launch model |

### Rollout principles
- Start where **demand overlap + terminal readiness** are highest
- Use explicit **go / no-go gates** before each expansion wave
- Keep product, operations, and finance reviews tightly linked

# Key risks and how we manage them

| Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Governance drift | Shared ownership can slow decisions | Clear reserved matters, 2+2 board, predefined escalation paths |
| Service inconsistency | Customer trust depends on reliability | Single service playbook, shared KPIs, launch certification before market go-live |
| Integration complexity | Data and routing handoffs can create friction | Phased tech integration, common API layer, dedicated PMO |
| Channel conflict | Teams may compete for the same accounts | Account rules of engagement and joint commercial planning |
| Volume shortfall in early markets | Density is critical to economics | Stage-gated rollout, minimum volume thresholds, flexible carrier model |
| Compliance / labor exposure | Local operations create execution risk | FastFreight-led compliance framework with JV audit rights |

### Risk view
The core risks are **manageable and operational**, not strategic.  
They improve with disciplined governance and phased rollout.
